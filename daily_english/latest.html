🗣 Oral Practice  主题：The Principal-Agent Problem

❓ Interview Question
Can you explain the principal-agent problem and discuss how it manifests in corporate governance? What mechanisms have been proposed to align the incentives of agents with those of principals?
📝 Reference Answer

The principal-agent problem arises whenever one party—the agent—is hired to act on behalf of another—the principal—but their objectives and incentives are not perfectly aligned. In corporate governance, shareholders (principals) delegate operational authority to professional managers (agents). Because managers possess superior information about their own actions and effort levels, they may pursue private interests—such as empire-building, excessive perquisites, or career risk aversion—at the expense of shareholder value. This informational gap is called information asymmetry.

Several mechanisms have been developed to mitigate this misalignment. Performance-based compensation—stock options, equity grants, and earnings-linked bonuses—ties managerial wealth to firm performance, though it can inadvertently incentivize short-termism or earnings manipulation. Board oversight provides an independent monitoring layer, but boards risk being captured by incumbent management. Concentrated ownership, where large institutional investors actively engage with management, reduces agency slack. Debt covenants constrain managerial discretion by imposing binding financial conditions. Finally, the market for corporate control—through hostile takeovers—disciplines underperforming managers by threatening their position.

No single mechanism is a panacea. Effective governance combines incentive alignment, robust monitoring, and market discipline. The persistence of agency costs in even well-governed firms suggests that information asymmetry is fundamentally difficult to eliminate, making institutional design an ongoing economic challenge.

💡 Theoretical Principle: Scaling Laws in Deep Learning
Scaling Laws, formalized by Kaplan et al. (2020) at OpenAI, describe a power-law relationship between model performance and three key variables: model parameter count, training data volume, and compute budget. As each resource scales up by an order of magnitude, test loss decreases predictably—an empirical regularity that has guided the development of every major frontier model since GPT-3. Critically, scaling laws reveal that compute, data, and model size must be scaled together in specific ratios (later refined by Hoffmann et al.'s "Chinchilla" work) to achieve optimal performance per FLOP. This principle explains why labs continue investing in larger training runs: performance improvements at scale remain largely predictable, making the economics of frontier AI development tractable. However, there is ongoing debate about whether scaling alone can achieve general intelligence or whether architectural innovations and reasoning enhancements—like the "thinking" mode in Gemini 2.5—represent a necessary complementary dimension of progress.

💡 Theoretical Principle: The Taylor Rule & Interest Rate Transmission Mechanism
The Taylor Rule, proposed by economist John Taylor in 1993, offers a formula for setting the nominal short-term interest rate based on two key deviations: the gap between actual and target inflation, and the gap between actual and potential GDP (the output gap). Specifically, it prescribes raising rates when inflation exceeds target or when the economy is overheating, and cutting when the opposite holds. The rule has become a benchmark for evaluating central bank policy, even though the Fed does not follow it mechanically. The interest rate transmission mechanism describes how changes in the federal funds rate ripple through the economy: lower rates reduce borrowing costs for businesses and households, stimulate investment and consumption, depreciate the currency (boosting exports), and lift asset prices—all of which collectively support aggregate demand. Conversely, tightening raises the cost of capital, slowing credit growth and cooling inflationary pressure. The challenge for policymakers is that monetary policy operates with long and variable lags, typically 12–18 months, making calibration under uncertainty a persistent difficulty.

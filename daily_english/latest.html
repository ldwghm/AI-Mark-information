Question
"Can you explain what yield curve inversion means, why it has historically been considered a reliable recession signal, and what are the main limitations of relying on it as a predictive indicator?"
Reference Answer

A yield curve plots interest rates of government bonds — typically U.S. Treasuries — across different maturities, from 3 months to 30 years. Under normal conditions, longer-term bonds yield more than short-term ones, reflecting greater uncertainty over time, producing an upward-sloping curve.

Yield curve inversion occurs when short-term rates exceed long-term rates — most closely watched via the 10-year minus 2-year Treasury spread turning negative. This inversion has preceded each of the last seven U.S. recessions since the 1960s, earning it status as one of the most reliable leading economic indicators.

The transmission mechanism works through credit markets: banks borrow short-term and lend long-term, so an inverted curve compresses their net interest margins, discouraging lending and tightening overall credit conditions. The curve also captures market expectations — when investors anticipate a future rate cut in response to a weakening economy, they bid up long-term bond prices, pushing long yields down below short-term rates.

However, its limitations are significant. Lead times between inversion and actual recession range from 6 to 24 months, making precise timing very unreliable. Structural shifts — including central bank quantitative easing programs, global demand for safe assets, and regulatory changes to bank capital requirements — may have altered the curve's classical predictive relationship in recent cycles.

Theoretical Principle
The Taylor Rule
The Taylor Rule, introduced by economist John B. Taylor in 1993, is a monetary policy guideline that prescribes how a central bank should set its short-term interest rate in response to deviations of inflation from its target and deviations of real GDP from potential output. The classic formula suggests the nominal interest rate should equal the neutral real rate plus current inflation, plus one-half the inflation gap (actual minus target inflation), plus one-half the output gap (actual minus potential GDP). In practice, central banks use the Taylor Rule as a benchmark rather than a strict constraint — when actual policy rates deviate significantly from the rule's prescription, it signals either that policymakers see risks the rule doesn't capture, or that they are deliberately running an accommodative or restrictive stance. The Fed's current pause in rate adjustments can be interpreted through this lens: with inflation modestly above target but output close to potential, the rule yields only moderate justification for immediate cuts, consistent with the Fed's data-dependent messaging.

Question
Explain the CAP theorem. What trade-offs does it impose on distributed system designers, and how do modern systems like Cassandra or Zookeeper navigate these constraints?
Reference Answer

The CAP theorem, originally conjectured by Eric Brewer in 2000 and formally proved by Gilbert and Lynch in 2002, states that a distributed system can guarantee at most two of three properties simultaneously: Consistency, Availability, and Partition tolerance.

Consistency means every read returns the most recent write or an error. Availability guarantees every request receives a response — though not necessarily reflecting the latest data. Partition tolerance means the system continues functioning even when network failures prevent some nodes from communicating.

Because network partitions are an unavoidable reality in any distributed system, designers must effectively choose between CP and AP. CA systems — those sacrificing partition tolerance — are only feasible in single-node architectures and are impractical in real-world distributed deployments.

Apache Zookeeper and HBase exemplify CP systems: they halt writes during a partition to prevent inconsistency, making them ideal for leader election and distributed coordination. Apache Cassandra and Amazon DynamoDB are AP systems: they accept writes during partitions and reconcile divergent states afterward using eventual consistency.

It is also worth mentioning the PACELC theorem, which extends CAP by noting that even without partitions, systems must trade off between latency and consistency. This enriches the CAP framework and better captures the full design space of real-world distributed databases.

Theoretical Principle  —  Scaling Laws (Kaplan et al.)
Neural scaling laws, formally established by Kaplan et al. at OpenAI in 2020, describe a remarkably consistent power-law relationship between a model's performance and three key resources: the number of parameters, the volume of training data, and the total compute budget. As each factor scales up by an order of magnitude, model loss decreases in a smooth and predictable manner — a finding that transformed AI research from art to engineering. The subsequent Chinchilla scaling laws (Hoffmann et al., 2022) refined this insight, demonstrating that optimal performance requires balanced scaling of both model size and training tokens simultaneously; smaller but compute-optimally trained models like Chinchilla outperformed the much larger GPT-3. These laws underpin the industry's multi-billion-dollar investment in frontier model training and justify why each successive model generation delivers capability improvements that closely match theoretical predictions.

Theoretical Principle  —  The Taylor Rule
The Taylor Rule, introduced by economist John B. Taylor in 1993, provides a prescriptive formula for setting a central bank's short-term interest rate based on prevailing macroeconomic conditions. In its classic form, the rule states that the nominal interest rate should equal the natural real rate of interest, plus the current inflation rate, plus 1.5 times the inflation gap (actual minus target), plus 0.5 times the output gap (actual GDP minus potential GDP). When inflation exceeds the target, the rule prescribes raising rates aggressively — by more than the inflation overshoot itself — to restore price stability; this property, known as the Taylor Principle, is essential for preventing self-fulfilling inflation spirals. Conversely, when output falls below potential during a recession, the rule calls for rate cuts to stimulate demand. While the Federal Reserve does not mechanically follow the Taylor Rule, its underlying logic — balancing the dual mandate of price stability and maximum employment — closely mirrors the Fed's stated policy framework, making it an indispensable analytical lens for interpreting central bank decisions.

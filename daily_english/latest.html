🗣️ Oral Practice

Question
Can you explain the Principal-Agent Problem? Why does it arise in organisations, and what mechanisms can firms or institutions use to mitigate it? Give a concrete real-world example.
Reference Answer

The Principal-Agent Problem emerges whenever one party — the principal — delegates decision-making authority to another party — the agent — whose interests may not perfectly align with their own. The root cause is information asymmetry: the agent typically knows more about their own effort and capabilities than the principal can observe, creating scope for opportunistic behaviour.

This misalignment manifests in two classic forms. Moral hazard occurs after a contract is signed, when the agent reduces effort because the principal cannot monitor every action (e.g., a salaried employee doing the minimum). Adverse selection occurs before contracting, when hidden characteristics lead to poor matching (e.g., an investment bank hiring a trader who privately knows their own risk appetite).

Firms combat this through several mechanisms: incentive alignment — tying agent compensation to outcomes (stock options for CEOs link their wealth to shareholder value); monitoring and auditing — board oversight, performance reviews; and reputation effects — repeated games where agents value long-run relationships. A textbook example is CEO pay: equity-based compensation converts the agent partly into a principal, reducing the gap between self-interest and firm value.

No mechanism eliminates the problem entirely — residual agency costs always remain — but well-designed contracts and governance structures substantially reduce them.

Theoretical Principle
Schumpeter's Creative Destruction & General-Purpose Technology (GPT) Theory

Joseph Schumpeter argued that capitalism advances through waves of innovation in which new technologies "destroy" existing industries and business models while creating new ones — a process he called creative destruction. Complementing this, economists like Bresnahan and Trajtenberg defined General-Purpose Technologies (GPTs) as innovations — steam engines, electricity, computers — that permeate the entire economy, enabling productivity gains only after widespread co-invention of complementary organisational changes. AI, and especially autonomous reasoning agents, is increasingly treated as the latest GPT: its economic impact depends not just on the model itself but on how firms restructure processes, retrain workers, and redesign incentive systems around it. The transition period can be painful — existing jobs are disrupted before new roles fully materialise — but historical GPT diffusions suggest long-run output and welfare gains. The key policy challenge is managing the transition equitably.

Theoretical Principle
Taylor Rule & Central Bank Reaction Function

The Taylor Rule, formulated by economist John Taylor in 1993, provides a prescriptive framework for how a central bank should set its policy interest rate: the rate should rise when inflation exceeds target or when output is above potential (an overheated economy), and fall when either condition is reversed. Formally, the rule states that the nominal rate equals the neutral real rate plus inflation plus coefficients on the inflation gap and the output gap. The Fed's current stance — holding rates despite slowing inflation — reflects a data-dependent application of this rule under uncertainty: policymakers want more confirmation that disinflation is durable before easing, since cutting prematurely risks re-igniting price pressures. Crucially, the rule also anchors market expectations: when investors understand the central bank's reaction function, forward guidance (communicating future intentions) can itself be a monetary policy tool, shaping long-term interest rates and asset prices even without an immediate rate change.

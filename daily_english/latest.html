🎙 Oral Practice

Question
Can you explain the concept of moral hazard and provide a real-world example of how it manifests in financial markets or corporate governance? How do organizations or regulators typically address it?
Reference Answer

Moral hazard refers to the tendency of an individual or institution to take on excessive risk when they do not bear the full consequences of that risk — typically because another party absorbs the downside. The term originates in the insurance industry, where policyholders insured against loss might behave more recklessly than if fully exposed to risk.

The classic modern example is the 2008 global financial crisis. Large banks originated and securitized subprime mortgages, offloading credit risk to investors through instruments like CDOs. Because originators bore little residual risk, they had weakened incentives to enforce lending standards. When the housing market collapsed, the entire financial system faced systemic failure. Governments then bailed out institutions deemed "too big to fail," inadvertently compounding future moral hazard — banks anticipated rescue, encouraging further risk-taking.

In corporate governance, moral hazard also surfaces in the principal-agent relationship: executives (agents) may pursue short-term earnings or personal perquisites at shareholders' (principals') expense, since managers are insulated from firm-level losses.

Remedies include skin-in-the-game requirements (equity retention by loan originators), performance-linked pay with clawback provisions, regulatory capital buffers (Basel III), and transparent disclosure. The underlying goal is to realign incentives so that risk-takers internalize the costs of their decisions.

Theoretical Principle: General Purpose Technology (GPT) Theory
General Purpose Technology (GPT) Theory, developed by economists Bresnahan and Trajtenberg, holds that certain foundational technologies — electricity, the steam engine, the internet — eventually permeate the entire economy, enabling a cascade of complementary innovations that drive long-run productivity growth. AI is widely regarded as the current GPT candidate. The theory predicts a characteristic "productivity paradox" in early adoption phases: firms invest heavily but measured productivity lags because organizational structures, skills, and business processes must co-evolve with the technology. Only after these complementary adjustments occur does aggregate output visibly accelerate. The mass deployment of AI agents represents exactly this co-evolution moment — firms are restructuring workflows, retraining employees, and redesigning incentive systems to extract GPT-level gains from large language model infrastructure.

Theoretical Principle: Yield Curve and Expectations Theory
The Expectations Theory of the term structure holds that long-term interest rates are essentially an average of current and expected future short-term rates. When investors anticipate rate cuts, demand for longer-dated bonds rises, pushing their yields down and flattening or inverting the yield curve. A "bull flattening" — where both ends of the curve fall but the short end falls faster — is characteristic of an expected Fed pivot from tightening to easing. Historically, the yield curve's shape has been one of the most reliable leading indicators of economic cycles: an inverted curve (short rates above long rates) has preceded each of the last seven U.S. recessions by roughly 12–18 months, though the signal is not infallible. Bond duration and convexity govern how sensitively a portfolio responds to these yield shifts, making curve dynamics central to both fixed-income investing and macroeconomic forecasting.

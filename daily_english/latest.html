Topic: Finance  ·  CAPM and Its Assumptions

Question

Can you explain the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM), walk through its key assumptions, and discuss the empirical limitations that have motivated the development of multi-factor asset pricing models?

Reference Answer

The Capital Asset Pricing Model, developed independently by Sharpe, Lintner, and Mossin in the 1960s, is a cornerstone of modern finance. It describes the linear relationship between an asset's expected return and its systematic risk, quantified by beta (β). The formula is: E(Ri) = Rf + βi × [E(Rm) − Rf], where Rf is the risk-free rate and the bracketed term is the market risk premium.

CAPM rests on several simplifying assumptions: investors are rational and mean-variance optimizers; markets are frictionless with no taxes or transaction costs; all investors share homogeneous expectations about returns; they can borrow and lend unlimited amounts at the risk-free rate; and all assets are marketable and perfectly divisible.

In practice, these assumptions rarely hold. Empirical anomalies — the size effect (small-cap outperformance), the value premium (low price-to-book stocks beating the market), and momentum — persistently contradict CAPM's single-factor prediction. Fama and French responded with a three-factor model incorporating size (SMB) and value (HML) factors, later expanded to five factors by adding profitability and investment. Carhart added a momentum factor.

Despite these shortcomings, CAPM remains foundational: it established the critical distinction between systematic risk — which the market rewards — and idiosyncratic risk — which can be diversified away and commands no premium. This insight underpins modern portfolio construction and cost-of-equity estimation in corporate finance.

Theoretical Principle: Scaling Laws in Deep Learning

Scaling laws, formalized by Kaplan et al. at OpenAI, describe predictable power-law relationships between a model's performance and three variables: the number of parameters, the size of the training dataset, and the amount of compute used. As each of these scales up, model loss decreases in a smooth, predictable fashion across many orders of magnitude. This insight has driven the "bigger is better" paradigm in large language and multimodal model development, because researchers can forecast performance improvements before investing in expensive training runs. However, scaling laws also reveal diminishing returns: doubling parameters yields less than a doubling of capability, which pushes the field toward architectural innovations — such as mixture-of-experts (MoE) designs and improved data curation — alongside raw scale, to sustain progress cost-effectively.

Theoretical Principle: The Taylor Rule

The Taylor Rule, proposed by economist John Taylor in 1993, is a prescriptive monetary policy guideline that recommends a central bank's target interest rate based on two gaps: the deviation of inflation from its target, and the deviation of real GDP from its potential (the output gap). Formally: i = r* + π + 0.5(π − π*) + 0.5(y − ȳ), where i is the nominal rate, r* is the equilibrium real rate, π is inflation, π* is the inflation target, and (y − ȳ) is the output gap. When inflation runs above target, the rule prescribes raising rates more than one-for-one — the "Taylor principle" — to ensure the real rate actually rises and dampens demand. Central banks use the Taylor Rule as a benchmark rather than a strict formula, since deviations from it offer transparency about when and why policymakers are exercising discretionary judgment, such as tolerating higher inflation temporarily to avoid destabilizing financial markets or deepening a recession.

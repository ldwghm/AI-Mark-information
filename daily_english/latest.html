Question
"Can you explain what yield curve inversion means and why economists and investors use it as a leading indicator for recession?"
Reference Answer

The yield curve plots the relationship between interest rates — or yields — and the time to maturity for bonds of equal credit quality, typically U.S. Treasury securities. Under normal conditions, longer-term bonds carry higher yields than shorter-term ones, compensating investors for the added risk of committing capital over a longer horizon. This produces the familiar upward-sloping curve.

Yield curve inversion occurs when short-term yields exceed long-term yields. The most closely watched spread is between the 2-year and 10-year Treasury notes. This inversion signals that investors expect interest rates to fall in the future — which typically happens when markets anticipate an economic slowdown. When the Federal Reserve aggressively raises short-term rates to combat inflation, short-term yields spike while long-term yields remain subdued as bond markets price in eventual rate cuts ahead.

Historically, the 2–10 spread has inverted before every U.S. recession since the 1970s, typically with a lead time of 12 to 24 months. The inversion also has a direct real-economy mechanism: banks borrow short-term and lend long-term, so an inverted curve compresses their net interest margins, constraining credit creation and slowing economic activity.

Critics note that the relationship has occasionally produced false positives and that the lag time varies. Nevertheless, the yield curve remains one of the most widely cited and historically reliable recession predictors in macroeconomics.

Theoretical Principle · The Taylor Rule
The Taylor Rule (泰勒规则), formulated by Stanford economist John Taylor in 1993, prescribes how central banks should set short-term interest rates in response to two deviations: the gap between actual inflation and the target rate, and the gap between actual economic output and potential output. A critical insight is the Taylor principle: when inflation rises by 1%, the central bank should raise its policy rate by more than 1% — ensuring that real interest rates actually increase, which cools demand. Conversely, a large negative output gap (recession) calls for rate cuts to stimulate borrowing and spending. When persistent services inflation prevents inflation from returning to target, the Taylor Rule implies that rates must stay higher for longer — precisely the signal Fed officials communicated. Bond markets quickly absorb such signals, repricing yields upward as the probability of near-term cuts falls. Understanding the Taylor Rule allows investors to better anticipate monetary policy moves and their impact on asset prices across equities, bonds, and currencies.

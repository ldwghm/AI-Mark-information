🗣️ Oral Practice — Principal-Agent Problem

Interview Question
"In corporate governance, the principal-agent problem is often cited as a major source of inefficiency. Can you explain what this problem is, why it arises, and describe at least two real-world mechanisms companies use to mitigate it?"
Reference Answer

The principal-agent problem arises when one party — the principal — delegates decision-making authority to another party — the agent — whose interests do not perfectly align with theirs. Because the agent possesses private information and acts on their own behalf, they may make decisions that benefit themselves at the principal's expense. This information asymmetry is the root cause of the problem.

In corporate governance, shareholders (principals) hire managers (agents) to run the firm. A manager might pursue empire-building acquisitions to boost their own prestige, or avoid risky but profitable projects to protect their job security — actions that destroy shareholder value.

Companies use several mechanisms to mitigate this conflict. First, incentive alignment: tying executive compensation to long-term stock performance through equity grants or stock options so managers profit when shareholders profit. Second, monitoring: independent boards of directors, external auditors, and activist investors all serve as watchdogs who scrutinize management decisions. Third, market discipline: the threat of hostile takeovers creates pressure for managers to maximize firm value, since underperforming companies become acquisition targets where incumbent management is typically replaced.

Each mechanism involves trade-offs between cost, effectiveness, and potential unintended consequences — for example, stock options can incentivize excessive risk-taking.

Scaling Laws in machine learning describe the empirical relationship between model performance and the scale of compute, data, and parameters — typically following smooth power-law functions. First systematically quantified by researchers at OpenAI in 2020, these laws suggest that larger models trained on more data with greater compute consistently achieve lower loss, often predictably so. This insight shifted AI research strategy away from architectural innovation toward aggressive scaling. However, scaling laws also reveal diminishing returns: to halve the loss, one must roughly increase compute by an order of magnitude. Theoretical Principle: The Taylor Rule
The Taylor Rule, formulated by economist John Taylor in 1993, provides a prescriptive framework for setting short-term interest rates based on economic conditions. The rule states that the nominal interest rate should equal the neutral real rate plus current inflation, plus a weighted response to the inflation gap (actual minus target) and the output gap (actual GDP minus potential GDP). When inflation is above target or output is above potential, the rule prescribes higher rates to cool demand; when both are below target, it prescribes rate cuts to stimulate. Central banks do not follow the Taylor Rule mechanically, but it serves as a useful benchmark for assessing whether monetary policy is hawkish or dovish relative to fundamentals. The Fed's current "data-dependent" language reflects a discretionary approach that weighs the same variables the Taylor Rule formalizes.

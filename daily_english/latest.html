Question
Can you explain the principal-agent problem and discuss how modern companies design compensation structures to mitigate it?
Reference Answer
The principal-agent problem arises whenever one party — the agent — is hired to act on behalf of another — the principal — but their interests diverge. Classic examples include shareholders (principals) delegating decision-making authority to corporate managers (agents), or employers relying on employees to exert effort that is costly to observe. The central difficulty is information asymmetry: the agent typically knows more about their own actions and effort level than the principal does, creating the risk of opportunistic behavior.

Companies have developed several mechanisms to realign incentives. First, performance-based compensation — stock options, restricted share units, and profit-sharing bonuses — directly links agent rewards to outcomes the principal cares about. However, this forces risk onto agents, and overly noisy performance metrics can undermine motivation. Second, monitoring mechanisms such as internal audits, board oversight, and management information systems reduce information asymmetry, though they are costly to operate. Third, reputation effects in competitive managerial labor markets naturally discipline agents who behave opportunistically, since past misconduct is observable by future employers. Fourth, long-term vesting schedules on equity grants discourage myopic behavior and encourage alignment with sustainable value creation.

Holmström's informativeness principle formalizes this: any signal that provides additional information about agent effort should be incorporated into the compensation contract. In practice, the optimal contract balances risk-sharing against incentive intensity — the more risk-averse the agent, the more the design should shield them from exogenous noise while still preserving accountability.

🔬 Theoretical Principle: The Scaling Hypothesis
The Scaling Hypothesis — closely related to the empirical observation of Moore's Law in semiconductor development — posits that the capabilities of large language models improve predictably and continuously as three key resources scale up: the number of model parameters, the volume of training data, and the amount of compute used during training. Unlike Moore's Law, which described a physical engineering regularity, the scaling hypothesis reflects a statistical learning phenomenon: larger models trained on more diverse data develop emergent capabilities not present in smaller predecessors. This principle has driven hundreds of billions of dollars in AI infrastructure investment, as firms believe that raw scale reliably translates into qualitative capability gains. Critics note, however, that the hypothesis may face diminishing returns as data quality ceilings are hit and inference costs climb, suggesting future progress will require architectural innovation rather than simple scale.

🔬 Theoretical Principle: The Taylor Rule & Monetary Transmission Mechanism
The Taylor Rule, formulated by economist John Taylor in 1993, provides a systematic prescription for how central banks should set short-term interest rates based on two deviations: inflation from its target rate and real GDP from its potential (the "output gap"). When inflation runs above target or the economy overheats, the rule prescribes rate hikes; when output falls below potential or inflation undershoots, rate cuts are warranted. Central banks rarely follow it mechanically, but it serves as a useful benchmark for assessing whether monetary policy is accommodative or restrictive. The monetary transmission mechanism describes how these rate decisions propagate through the economy — via the credit channel (changing borrowing costs for households and firms), the asset price channel (influencing equity valuations and wealth effects), the exchange rate channel (affecting export competitiveness), and the expectations channel (shaping inflation expectations through forward guidance). The effectiveness of transmission depends critically on central bank credibility: if markets trust the inflation target, even the announcement of future cuts can lower long-term yields and stimulate investment today.

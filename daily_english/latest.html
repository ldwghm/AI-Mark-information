🗣️ Oral Practice — Principal-Agent Problem

❓ Interview Question
Can you explain the principal-agent problem? How does it manifest in corporate governance, and what mechanisms are commonly used to mitigate it?
📝 Reference Answer

The principal-agent problem arises whenever one party — the principal — delegates decision-making authority to another party — the agent — but the two have divergent interests and asymmetric information. Because the agent acts in their own self-interest, outcomes may systematically deviate from what the principal would prefer, generating what economists call agency costs.

In corporate governance, the textbook example is the relationship between shareholders (principals) and professional managers (agents). Shareholders seek long-run value maximization, while managers may prioritize short-term earnings, personal perquisites, or excessive risk-aversion to protect their jobs. This misalignment is compounded by information asymmetry: managers possess far deeper knowledge of operations than any dispersed shareholder can realistically obtain.

Several mechanisms are deployed to contain these costs:

  • Incentive alignment — equity compensation, stock options, and performance bonuses tie managerial rewards to shareholder returns.
  • Monitoring — independent boards, external auditors, and mandatory disclosures reduce information asymmetry.
  • Market discipline — hostile-takeover threats and competitive executive labor markets impose external accountability.
  • Contractual constraints — debt covenants and shareholder agreements restrict high-risk or self-serving managerial decisions.

As Jensen and Meckling (1976) argued, some residual agency cost is unavoidable in any firm with a separation of ownership and control. The practical goal of governance design is not elimination but minimization — reducing waste while preserving the managerial initiative that drives firm performance.

💡 Theoretical Principle: Scaling Laws in AI (Neural Scaling Laws)
Neural scaling laws, first rigorously quantified by Kaplan et al. (OpenAI, 2020), describe a predictable power-law relationship between model performance and three key resources: the number of model parameters, the volume of training data, and the amount of compute used during training. As each resource scales up, model loss falls smoothly and predictably — meaning engineers can forecast capability improvements before training a single model. This insight transformed AI development from an empirical craft into a more systematic engineering discipline: labs now plan compute budgets years in advance and use scaling laws to decide when a larger run is worth the investment. However, scaling laws also imply diminishing returns beyond certain thresholds, and researchers increasingly debate whether pure scale can sustain the pace of progress or whether architectural and data-quality innovations will become the binding constraint.

💡 Theoretical Principle: The Taylor Rule
The Taylor Rule, formulated by economist John Taylor in 1993, provides a systematic framework for setting short-term interest rates based on two deviations: how far actual inflation is from the central bank's target, and how far real output is from its potential (the "output gap"). When inflation runs above target or the economy overheats, the rule prescribes raising rates more than one-for-one with the inflation deviation — a property known as the Taylor Principle — to anchor expectations and cool demand. Conversely, when growth slumps, the rule calls for rate cuts to stimulate borrowing and investment. Although the Fed does not mechanically follow the formula, the Taylor Rule serves as a crucial benchmark for evaluating whether monetary policy is appropriately tight or loose, and deviations from it often spark academic and market debate about policy credibility. The current hawkish stance reflects a Taylor-consistent response to an inflation overshoot, even amid moderate growth concerns.

Question
Can you explain the CAP theorem and discuss the real-world trade-offs when designing distributed systems? Please give concrete examples of systems that make different choices along the CAP spectrum, and explain why those choices make sense for their use cases.
Reference Answer

The CAP theorem, formulated by Eric Brewer in 2000 and formally proven by Gilbert and Lynch in 2002, states that a distributed data store can guarantee at most two of three properties simultaneously: Consistency (every read returns the most recent write), Availability (every request receives a non-error response), and Partition Tolerance (the system keeps functioning despite network partitions that drop messages between nodes).

In practice, network partitions are an unavoidable reality in any distributed system, so partition tolerance is non-negotiable. This forces architects to choose between consistency and availability during a partition event. Systems like HBase and Apache ZooKeeper prioritize CP — they refuse requests rather than return stale data. Conversely, systems like Cassandra and Amazon DynamoDB prioritize AP — they remain accessible but use eventual consistency, where replicas converge asynchronously over time.

The PACELC theorem extends this thinking by noting that even without partitions, there is a latency–consistency trade-off. Strong consistency requires cross-node coordination, which adds latency, while eventual consistency allows reads from the nearest replica at lower latency.

Practical decisions depend on the domain: banking and payment systems demand CP to prevent double-spending or inconsistent balances, whereas social media timelines and product recommendation feeds can tolerate temporary inconsistency and benefit from AP's higher availability and lower latency.

Theoretical Principle
Wright's Law (Learning Curve Theory)
Wright's Law, originally observed in aircraft manufacturing, states that for every cumulative doubling of units produced, costs fall by a constant percentage. Applied to AI compute, it predicts that as the total amount of AI inference workload doubles globally — driven by adoption — the cost per inference continuously declines. This virtuous cycle means that hardware manufacturers, cloud providers, and model optimization researchers all invest more as the market grows, further driving down cost. The dramatic reduction in model inference costs we see today is a textbook manifestation of this learning curve: more usage generates revenue for R&D, producing better chips and algorithms that reduce cost, which in turn enables even broader deployment. Unlike Moore's Law (which describes transistor density doubling), Wright's Law focuses on cumulative production volume as the driver of cost reduction.

Theoretical Principle
Uncovered Interest Rate Parity (UIP)
Uncovered Interest Rate Parity (UIP) is a foundational concept in international finance stating that the difference in nominal interest rates between two countries should equal the expected change in the exchange rate between their currencies. In theory, when the U.S. offers higher interest rates than Country X, capital should flow into U.S. assets, appreciating the dollar until the expected future depreciation of the dollar exactly offsets the yield advantage — leaving investors indifferent between the two markets. In practice, however, this parity condition often fails to hold in the short run, a phenomenon called the "forward premium puzzle." High-interest-rate currencies tend to appreciate rather than depreciate as UIP predicts, enabling carry trade strategies where investors borrow in low-rate currencies and invest in high-rate ones. The current dollar strength and emerging market outflows represent both the initial UIP-driven capital flow and the carry trade dynamic playing out simultaneously.

🎤 Oral Practice  Finance – Yield Curve Inversion & Recession Signals

Question
Can you explain what a yield curve inversion is, why it happens, and why economists and investors treat it as a leading indicator of economic recession?
Reference Answer

A yield curve plots the interest rates of bonds with identical credit quality—typically U.S. Treasuries—across different maturities, ranging from one month to thirty years. Under normal conditions the curve slopes upward: investors demand a higher yield for longer maturities because they are tying up capital for longer and bearing greater uncertainty. An inversion occurs when short-term yields exceed long-term yields, flipping this relationship.

The most widely watched measure is the 2-year / 10-year Treasury spread. When this spread turns negative, it has preceded every U.S. recession since the 1960s, with a typical lead time of 6 to 24 months. Several mechanisms explain this predictive power. First, long-term yields reflect market expectations of future short-term rates; if investors expect the Federal Reserve to cut rates aggressively later—because the economy will weaken—they accept low long-term yields today. Second, inversion compresses net interest margins for banks, which fund themselves at short-term rates and lend at long-term rates. Squeezed profitability leads banks to tighten credit, reducing investment and consumption. Third, the signal can be self-fulfilling: businesses that observe an inverted curve may preemptively cut hiring and capital expenditure.

Importantly, inversion is probabilistic, not deterministic. The 2022–2023 inversion was one of the deepest on record, yet the U.S. avoided an immediate recession, leading economists to debate whether quantitative easing has distorted the bond market and weakened the indicator's reliability in the modern era.

Theoretical Principle
Uncovered Interest Rate Parity (UIP)（无抛补利率平价理论）
Uncovered Interest Rate Parity (UIP) is a foundational concept in international finance. It holds that, in the absence of capital controls, the difference in nominal interest rates between two countries should equal the expected change in the exchange rate between their currencies. In other words, if the U.S. offers higher interest rates than Europe, investors will move capital into dollar-denominated assets, driving up the dollar—until the expected future depreciation of the dollar exactly offsets the interest rate advantage. In practice, UIP frequently fails to hold in the short run—a phenomenon known as the "forward premium puzzle"—because investors exhibit risk aversion, carry-trade behavior, and irrational momentum. However, the mechanism remains useful for explaining why sustained U.S. rate differentials attract global capital inflows, put upward pressure on the dollar, and simultaneously generate stress for emerging-market borrowers who hold dollar-denominated debt.

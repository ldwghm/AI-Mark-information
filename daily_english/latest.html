Question
"Can you explain the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) and its key assumptions? How does CAPM determine the expected return of an asset, and what are the practical limitations of the model?"
Reference Answer

The Capital Asset Pricing Model, or CAPM, is a foundational framework in finance that describes the relationship between systematic risk and expected return for an asset. The core formula is: E(Ri) = Rf + βi × (E(Rm) − Rf), where Rf is the risk-free rate, β measures the asset's sensitivity to market movements, and the term in brackets is the equity risk premium.

CAPM rests on several key assumptions: investors are rational and risk-averse, markets are efficient with no transaction costs or taxes, all investors share identical return expectations, and every investor can borrow or lend at the risk-free rate. Under these conditions, only systematic risk—undiversifiable market risk—is compensated by higher expected returns, because idiosyncratic risk can be eliminated through diversification.

In practice, however, CAPM faces several challenges. The model is a single-factor model and cannot explain return anomalies like the value premium or momentum effect. Beta is historically estimated and may not reflect future risk accurately. Additionally, the "true" market portfolio is unobservable (Roll's Critique). These limitations led to extensions such as Fama-French's three-factor and five-factor models, which add size and value factors to improve explanatory power.

Despite its limitations, CAPM remains widely used in cost-of-capital estimation, project valuation, and portfolio performance benchmarking because of its elegant simplicity.

Theoretical Principle  — The Taylor Rule
The Federal Reserve's rate deliberations are often analyzed through the lens of the Taylor Rule, introduced by economist John Taylor in 1993. The Taylor Rule provides a prescriptive formula for setting a central bank's short-term interest rate: the policy rate should equal the neutral real interest rate plus the current inflation rate, plus a weighted average of two "gaps" — the deviation of actual inflation from the central bank's target (the inflation gap) and the deviation of real GDP from potential GDP (the output gap). When inflation exceeds target, the rule prescribes higher rates to cool demand; when the economy is operating below potential, it calls for lower rates to stimulate activity. The rule serves as a useful benchmark, but central banks deviate from it routinely in practice — for example, maintaining near-zero rates during quantitative easing periods far longer than the formula would suggest — reflecting judgments about structural uncertainties and financial stability that a simple formula cannot fully capture.

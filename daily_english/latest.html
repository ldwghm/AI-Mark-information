Interview Question
Can you explain the principal-agent problem? How does it manifest in corporate governance, and what mechanisms are commonly used to mitigate agency costs?
Reference Answer

The principal-agent problem arises when one party (the principal) delegates decision-making authority to another (the agent), whose interests may not perfectly align with the principal's. Information asymmetry exacerbates this: the agent typically has superior knowledge of their own effort and actions compared to what the principal can observe.

In corporate governance, shareholders (principals) hire managers (agents) to run the firm. Managers may pursue private benefits — empire-building, excessive perquisites, or excessive risk aversion — rather than maximizing shareholder value. Jensen and Meckling (1976) categorized the resulting agency costs into monitoring costs, bonding costs, and residual loss.

Several mechanisms help mitigate these costs. First, performance-based compensation — stock options and equity grants — aligns managerial incentives with shareholder returns. Second, board oversight and independent directors provide monitoring, while activist shareholders impose external discipline. Third, the market for corporate control — the threat of hostile takeovers — motivates managers, since poor performance depresses share prices and invites acquisition. Fourth, debt financing reduces free cash flow and constrains managerial discretion, per Jensen's free cash flow hypothesis. Finally, reputational concerns in executive labor markets incentivize managers to build track records of value creation.

Despite these tools, agency problems are never fully eliminated. The optimal contract must balance risk-sharing and incentive provision, inevitably leaving residual agency costs.

Disruptive Innovation Theory, proposed by Harvard Business School professor Clayton Christensen, describes how new entrants initially target overlooked or low-end market segments with simpler, cheaper products before progressively moving upmarket to displace established incumbents. Early AI models were dismissed as "toys" by enterprise software vendors, mirroring the classic disruption pattern. Theoretical Principle
Taylor Rule & Monetary Policy Transmission Mechanism
The Taylor Rule, formulated by economist John Taylor in 1993, provides a prescriptive formula for setting central bank policy rates based on deviations of inflation from its target and output from its potential level. When inflation exceeds target or output exceeds potential, the rule prescribes higher rates; when below, it prescribes cuts. The monetary policy transmission mechanism describes how central bank rate decisions propagate through the economy: changes in the federal funds rate influence commercial bank lending rates, which affect business investment decisions, consumer credit costs, and exchange rates, ultimately feeding through to aggregate demand and price levels with a typical lag of 12–18 months. The yield curve — plotting interest rates across maturities — is a real-time market signal of where investors expect rates to be in the future, making it an important leading indicator of economic activity. A bull-flattening (long rates falling while short rates hold steady) typically reflects growing expectations of future monetary easing.

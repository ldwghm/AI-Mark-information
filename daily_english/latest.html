🎤 Oral Practice — CS Graduate Interview

Question
"Can you explain the CAP theorem and describe a real-world scenario where you had to make a trade-off between consistency and availability in a distributed system?"
Reference Answer

The CAP theorem, proposed by Eric Brewer in 2000, states that a distributed system can guarantee at most two of three properties simultaneously: Consistency, Availability, and Partition Tolerance. Since network partitions are inevitable in any real distributed system, the practical trade-off almost always comes down to choosing between consistency and availability.

A CP system (e.g., Apache ZooKeeper, HBase) prioritizes consistency: if a network partition occurs, some nodes will refuse to respond rather than return stale data. This is critical for use cases like distributed locks or financial ledgers where correctness is non-negotiable.

An AP system (e.g., DynamoDB, Cassandra) prioritizes availability: every node continues serving requests even during a partition, but different nodes may temporarily return different values. This suits scenarios like shopping cart services or social media feeds, where serving slightly stale data is acceptable and downtime is not.

In practice, engineers use eventual consistency models with tunable consistency levels — for instance, Cassandra's quorum reads — to shift along the CAP spectrum dynamically based on workload requirements. It is also important to note that the PACELC theorem extends CAP by accounting for latency trade-offs even without partitions.

Theoretical Principle: Scaling Laws (神经网络规模定律)
Scaling Laws, formalized by Kaplan et al. at OpenAI in 2020, describe a predictable power-law relationship between model performance and three factors: the number of model parameters, the size of the training dataset, and the amount of compute used. As each of these scales up, loss on language modeling tasks decreases smoothly and predictably. This empirical regularity gave AI labs a reliable roadmap: by investing more compute and data, they could forecast performance improvements before training even began. The subsequent Chinchilla paper (Hoffmann et al., 2022) refined these laws, showing that many existing large models were over-parameterized relative to their training data, and that a smaller model trained on more tokens is often more efficient. Scaling Laws have thus become the central organizing principle behind the rapid generational leaps seen in frontier AI models.

Theoretical Principle: The Taylor Rule (泰勒规则)
The Taylor Rule, proposed by economist John Taylor in 1993, provides a formula for central banks to set nominal interest rates based on two key deviations: the gap between actual inflation and the inflation target, and the output gap (the difference between actual and potential GDP). When inflation runs above target or the economy overheats, the rule prescribes raising rates; when inflation is below target or a recession looms, it calls for cuts. The rule embeds the principle that monetary policy should respond systematically and predictably rather than discretionarily. Central banks like the Federal Reserve do not mechanically follow the rule, but it serves as a benchmark against which actual policy decisions are measured and debated. During periods of uncertainty — such as post-pandemic inflation — the Taylor Rule helps analysts assess whether policy is "too tight" or "too loose" relative to economic fundamentals.

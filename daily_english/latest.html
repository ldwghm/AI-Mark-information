🎙️ Oral Practice — CS Graduate Interview

Question
Can you explain the differences among a process, a thread, and a coroutine? When would you choose one over the others, and what are the key trade-offs in terms of memory, scheduling, and concurrency?
Reference Answer

A process is an independent unit of execution with its own dedicated memory space, file descriptors, and OS resources. Because processes are isolated by the operating system, they communicate via IPC mechanisms such as pipes, sockets, or shared memory. This isolation makes them fault-tolerant—a crash in one process does not corrupt another—but context switching between processes is expensive due to full memory-map switches.

A thread is a lighter-weight unit of execution that shares the address space of its parent process. Multiple threads within the same process can read and write shared variables directly, which makes communication fast but introduces data-race risks that require synchronization primitives (mutexes, semaphores). The OS scheduler manages thread preemption, so threads are suitable for CPU-bound parallelism on multi-core systems—subject to the Global Interpreter Lock in CPython.

A coroutine (green thread / fiber) is a user-space concurrency primitive that explicitly yields control at defined suspension points via async/await or generator syntax. Because scheduling happens in user space, context switches are extremely cheap (no kernel involvement), making coroutines ideal for I/O-bound workloads such as web servers handling thousands of simultaneous connections. The trade-off is that coroutines are cooperative, not preemptive—a CPU-bound coroutine that never yields can starve others.

In summary: use processes for isolation and fault tolerance, threads for shared-memory parallelism on multi-core hardware, and coroutines for high-concurrency I/O workloads with minimal overhead.

Moore's Law & the S-Curve of Technology Adoption
Moore's Law originally predicted that transistor density on integrated circuits would double roughly every two years, driving exponential gains in compute at falling cost. While the physical law has slowed at the silicon level, its economic logic—that each generation of capability unlocks a new wave of commercial deployment—continues to hold for software-driven AI. Technology adoption typically follows an S-curve: a slow experimental phase, a steep growth phase as infrastructure matures and costs fall, and a saturation plateau. The current surge in enterprise AI-agent adoption fits the steep-growth segment of that curve: compute costs have dropped sufficiently, foundation-model quality has crossed enterprise reliability thresholds, and tooling (orchestration frameworks, observability platforms) has matured enough for mainstream IT organizations to deploy at scale.

Theoretical Principle
Yield Curve as a Recession Signal & the Expectations Hypothesis
The Expectations Hypothesis of the term structure holds that long-term interest rates represent the market's average expectation of future short-term rates plus a term premium. When investors expect future rates to fall—typically because they anticipate economic slowdown—long-term yields decline relative to short-term yields, compressing or inverting the curve. Empirically, an inverted or flat yield curve (2-year rate ≥ 10-year rate) has preceded every U.S. recession since the 1960s with a lag of roughly 12–18 months, making it one of the most closely watched leading indicators. The current flattening reflects a market that is simultaneously pricing in Fed policy staying tight in the near term while doubting the economy can sustain that tightness for long—a classic precursor dynamic. However, economists caution that correlation is not causation: the curve signals probability shifts, not certainty, and structural factors such as central bank balance-sheet operations can suppress long-end yields independently of growth expectations.

Topic: The Principal-Agent Problem

Question

"Please explain the principal-agent problem. Why does it arise, and what mechanisms do companies use to mitigate it? Illustrate your answer with a concrete example from corporate governance."

Reference Answer

The principal-agent problem emerges whenever one party — the principal — delegates authority or decision-making to another party — the agent — whose interests may diverge from their own. Because the agent typically possesses private information and cannot be monitored without cost, a condition economists call information asymmetry, the agent may exploit this informational advantage to serve personal objectives at the principal's expense.

The canonical corporate-governance example is the shareholder–executive relationship. Shareholders (principals) want executives (agents) to maximize long-run firm value. Executives, however, may prefer higher salaries, low personal risk, or the prestige of empire-building — all of which can erode shareholder returns.

Companies deploy several alignment mechanisms to contain what Jensen and Meckling called agency costs. First, incentive compensation: stock options and equity grants tie managerial payoffs directly to share price. Second, monitoring structures: independent boards, external auditors, and activist institutional investors provide oversight. Third, market discipline: the threat of hostile takeover pressures managers to stay efficient.

None of these solutions is perfect. Equity-based pay can encourage short-termism or excessive risk-taking, as the 2008 financial crisis vividly demonstrated. The ongoing challenge in mechanism design is constructing contracts that achieve incentive compatibility — ensuring rational agents voluntarily act in the principal's best interest because it is also in their own.

Theoretical Principle

The Scaling Hypothesis & Emergent Capabilities
The Scaling Hypothesis proposes that simply increasing a neural network's parameter count, training data, and compute — without fundamental architectural changes — yields qualitatively new capabilities that were absent at smaller scales. Researchers Kaplan et al. (2020) formalized this as a power-law relationship between compute and loss, suggesting predictable improvements. However, certain abilities — such as multi-step planning and instruction-following — appear to "emerge" discontinuously once models cross specific size thresholds. This emergent behavior is why each generation of large language models surprises practitioners: capabilities cannot always be extrapolated smoothly from smaller predecessors. The phenomenon also complicates AI safety research, because dangerous capabilities may appear suddenly rather than gradually, making pre-deployment evaluation harder.

Theoretical Principle

The Taylor Rule & Monetary Transmission Mechanism
The Taylor Rule, proposed by economist John Taylor in 1993, provides a formula for central banks to set short-term interest rates based on deviations of actual inflation from the target rate and of actual GDP from potential output. When inflation rises above target, the rule prescribes raising rates more than one-for-one — ensuring a real-rate increase that cools demand (the "Taylor principle"). The monetary transmission mechanism then describes the chain reaction: a change in the policy rate ripples through bank lending rates, mortgage costs, corporate bond yields, exchange rates, and ultimately aggregate demand and prices, typically with a 12–18 month lag. Rate-cut expectations, even before actual cuts occur, can stimulate asset prices and investment through the "expectation channel," which is why bond markets react sharply to shifts in Fed communication — a phenomenon sometimes called "forward guidance" effects.

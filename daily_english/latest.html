Question
"Can you explain the concept of game theory and discuss how it applies to real-world business strategy? Please give at least one concrete example."
Reference Answer

Game theory is a branch of mathematics and economics that models strategic interactions among rational agents, where the outcome for each participant depends on the choices made by all others. It provides a formal framework for analyzing competitive and cooperative situations in which decision-makers anticipate how rivals will respond.

In business, game theory is widely applied to pricing strategy, market entry decisions, and negotiation. The classic Prisoner's Dilemma illustrates how two competing firms may both choose to cut prices — even though mutual restraint would be collectively better — because each fears being undercut if it holds back. This insight explains persistent price wars in industries such as airlines and consumer electronics.

Another key concept is the Nash Equilibrium, a situation in which no player can benefit by unilaterally changing their strategy. For example, in a duopoly where two telecom operators set data plan prices, the equilibrium emerges when neither can profitably deviate from its current pricing given the other's choice.

Game theory also informs first-mover advantage analysis: a firm that commits early to large-scale investment signals credibility to rivals, discouraging market entry — a strategy known as a credible commitment. Overall, game theory transforms intuitive notions of competition into precise, actionable frameworks for strategic planning.

Scaling Laws (神经网络规模定律)
Scaling Laws, first rigorously documented by Kaplan et al. at OpenAI in 2020, describe a predictable power-law relationship between a language model's performance and three variables: the number of parameters, the volume of training data, and the amount of compute used. As each variable increases, model loss decreases in a smooth, log-linear fashion — meaning doubling compute yields a reliable, quantifiable improvement. This empirical regularity has driven the industry's strategy of building ever-larger models, because it offers a roadmap: if you have sufficient hardware and data, you can forecast the capability gain before training begins. Theoretical Principle
Taylor Rule (泰勒规则)
The Taylor Rule, proposed by economist John Taylor in 1993, is a prescriptive monetary policy guideline that suggests how central banks should set short-term interest rates in response to changes in inflation and output. The rule states that the nominal interest rate should equal the neutral real rate plus current inflation, plus one-half times the inflation gap (actual minus target), plus one-half times the output gap (actual minus potential GDP). When inflation runs above target, the rule calls for raising rates aggressively — often by more than the inflation increase itself, a property known as the Taylor Principle — to prevent expectations from becoming unanchored. Conversely, when the economy is below potential, the rule prescribes rate cuts to stimulate demand. The Fed's current "hold" stance reflects the tension embedded in the Taylor Rule: elevated tariff-driven inflation argues for tighter policy, while any weakening in the labor market argues for easing — leaving the central bank in a data-dependent wait-and-see posture until one signal clearly dominates.

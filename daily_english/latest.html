🎙️ Oral Practice — Economics & Management

💬 Interview Question

Can you explain the principal-agent problem? How does it arise in corporate governance, and what mechanisms do firms use to mitigate the associated risks?

📝 Reference Answer

The principal-agent problem arises whenever one party (the principal) delegates decision-making authority to another (the agent), yet their interests diverge and the principal cannot perfectly observe the agent's actions. The classic corporate example is the relationship between shareholders and managers: shareholders want to maximize firm value, while managers may pursue personal benefits — larger offices, empire-building acquisitions, or simply less effort — at shareholders' expense.

The root cause is twofold: information asymmetry (the agent knows more about their own effort than the principal does) and moral hazard (once hired, agents may shirk because full monitoring is costly). These frictions generate agency costs — expenditures on monitoring, bonding arrangements, and residual value lost despite these controls.

Firms deploy several mitigation mechanisms. Incentive alignment through stock options and performance-linked bonuses ties managerial wealth to shareholder returns, making the agent bear part of the downside. Board oversight — especially independent directors — provides a monitoring layer. Debt financing can discipline managers by forcing free cash flow commitment. External mechanisms such as hostile takeovers and managerial labor market competition further constrain opportunism. Despite these tools, agency costs can never be fully eliminated; the optimal contract merely minimizes their total impact.

💡 Theoretical Principle: Scaling Laws (Neural Scaling Laws)

Scaling Laws, formalized by Kaplan et al. (OpenAI, 2020), describe a remarkably predictable relationship: as the number of model parameters, training compute, and dataset size increase, model performance improves in a smooth, power-law fashion. This means that a tenfold increase in compute reliably yields a quantifiable reduction in loss, enabling labs to forecast model capability before training completes. The insight underpins the modern "bigger is better" paradigm — rather than algorithmic breakthroughs alone, raw scale has proven to be a dominant driver of performance. However, scaling laws also reveal diminishing returns at certain thresholds, which has prompted research into more efficient training regimes like mixture-of-experts (MoE) architectures and better data curation. The continued arms race between Google, OpenAI, and others is therefore fundamentally a competition over who can scale compute most efficiently and cost-effectively.

💡 Theoretical Principle: The Taylor Rule

The Taylor Rule, proposed by economist John Taylor in 1993, provides a simple formula for how central banks should set nominal interest rates: the recommended rate equals the neutral real rate plus current inflation, plus adjustments for deviations of inflation from its target and of GDP from its potential. When inflation runs above target, the rule prescribes higher rates to cool demand; when output falls below potential, it recommends lower rates to stimulate. The Fed does not mechanically follow the Taylor Rule, but it serves as a widely used benchmark to assess whether monetary policy is "too tight," "too loose," or roughly appropriate. In the current environment, with inflation still above the 2% target despite cooling from its 2022 peak, a Taylor Rule calculation would support the Fed's cautious posture of holding rates steady rather than cutting aggressively. The tension between the rule's prescription and market expectations for faster cuts illustrates how the Taylor Rule frames public debate over central bank credibility and forward guidance.

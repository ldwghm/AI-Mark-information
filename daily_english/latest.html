🎤 Oral Practice  — Economics & Management

❓ Question
Can you explain the principal-agent problem? What are its main causes, and what mechanisms can firms use to align the interests of principals and agents? Please give a real-world example.
📝 Reference Answer

The principal-agent problem arises when one party — the principal — delegates decision-making authority to another party — the agent — whose interests may not perfectly align with the principal's. This misalignment stems from two core issues: information asymmetry and divergent incentives. The agent typically possesses private information the principal cannot easily observe, creating opportunities for opportunistic behavior.

A classic example is the relationship between shareholders (principals) and corporate managers (agents). Shareholders want managers to maximize long-term firm value; managers, however, may prioritize short-term bonuses, excessive perks, or empire-building acquisitions that benefit themselves but destroy shareholder value — a phenomenon known as managerial entrenchment.

Firms deploy several mechanisms to mitigate this problem. Performance-based compensation — such as stock options and equity grants — ties managerial rewards to stock price, aligning interests. Monitoring mechanisms, including independent boards of directors, internal audits, and institutional investor oversight, reduce information asymmetry. Reputation effects and competitive labor markets also discipline agents: a manager with a track record of self-dealing faces reduced future employment prospects.

Despite these tools, perfect alignment is rarely achieved due to the inherent cost of monitoring. This is why corporate governance remains a central concern in both economics and management theory.

💡 Theoretical Principle: Yield Curve Theory & Expectations Hypothesis
The Expectations Hypothesis of the Term Structure holds that long-term interest rates are essentially the geometric average of expected future short-term rates. When the yield curve flattens — meaning the spread between short-term and long-term Treasury yields narrows — it signals that bond market participants expect short-term rates to remain elevated or decline only modestly in the future, reflecting reduced optimism about near-term monetary easing. A fully inverted yield curve (where short rates exceed long rates) has historically preceded every U.S. recession since the 1970s, making it one of the most closely watched leading economic indicators. The flattening observed in May 2026 suggests that, while markets do not yet anticipate an outright recession, growth expectations are being tempered as the "higher for longer" rate environment persists and weighs on credit-sensitive sectors such as real estate, small-cap equities, and leveraged buyouts.

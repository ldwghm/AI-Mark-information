Question
Can you explain the principal-agent problem? Please provide a concrete real-world example, discuss why information asymmetry makes it difficult to resolve, and outline the key mechanisms companies use to mitigate the associated risks.
Reference Answer

The principal-agent problem arises whenever one party — the principal — delegates decision-making authority to another party — the agent — but their interests are not perfectly aligned. Because the agent typically has better information about their own effort and capabilities than the principal does, information asymmetry allows the agent to act in self-interested ways that diverge from the principal's objectives, a phenomenon broadly termed moral hazard.

A classic example is the relationship between shareholders and corporate executives. Shareholders want to maximize long-run firm value, while managers may prioritize personal perks, job security, or empire-building through over-ambitious acquisitions. This misalignment can destroy shareholder value unless appropriate mechanisms are in place.

Companies address the problem through three main channels. First, incentive alignment: linking executive compensation to stock performance or profitability targets makes the agent share in the consequences of their decisions. Second, monitoring: independent boards of directors, external audits, and mandatory financial disclosures reduce information asymmetry. Third, reputation and career concerns: managers with long time horizons have an incentive to behave prudently because poor performance damages their professional reputation.

In financial markets, the same problem surfaces in fund management — portfolio managers may take on excessive risk because they capture upside gains but are insulated from losses. Regulatory responses include fiduciary duty requirements and performance clawback provisions. Ultimately, no single mechanism fully eliminates the problem; effective governance combines incentives, information, and institutional oversight.

Theoretical Principle: Scaling Laws in Large Language Models
Scaling Laws, empirically formalized by Kaplan et al. at OpenAI in 2020, describe how the performance of large neural networks improves as a predictable power-law function of three factors: model parameter count, training dataset size, and compute budget. Unlike Moore's Law — which governs hardware transistor density — Scaling Laws govern algorithmic capability, suggesting that doubling any of these three inputs yields consistent, measurable improvements in downstream task performance. This principle has reshaped AI industry strategy: companies with greater capital can secure compute-driven performance advantages, creating significant barriers to entry. However, the Chinchilla findings (DeepMind, 2022) refined the original laws, showing that many models had been over-parameterized and undertrained — optimal performance requires balancing both model size and data volume. Theoretical Principle: The Taylor Rule & Monetary Policy Transmission
The Taylor Rule, introduced by economist John B. Taylor in 1993, provides a prescriptive formula for setting nominal interest rates based on two key gaps: the deviation of current inflation from its target (typically 2%), and the deviation of real GDP from its potential level (the output gap). When inflation exceeds target, the rule prescribes raising rates aggressively — with a coefficient greater than one on the inflation gap (the "Taylor Principle") — to avoid negative real interest rates that would further stimulate demand. This tightening mechanism transmits to the broader economy by raising borrowing costs for firms and households, dampening investment and consumption, and — via asset pricing — reducing equity valuations as higher discount rates compress present values of future cash flows. The relationship between rate expectations and equity prices is particularly direct: growth stocks with earnings concentrated in the future are most sensitive to discount rate changes, explaining the asymmetric pressure observed on technology indices when rate-cut timelines extend. While the Fed does not mechanically follow the Taylor Rule, it remains the most widely cited benchmark for assessing whether monetary policy is loose, neutral, or restrictive.
